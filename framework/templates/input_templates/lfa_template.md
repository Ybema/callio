# Project definition

## Background

* + 1. Problem origin
    2. Current methods or approach to solve the problem
    3. Limitations of current approaches
    4. Proposed solution and its advantages

## Overall goal

The ultimate result to which the project is contributing.

Framing a quality ‘Overall Goal’:

* + It is a state of being that is more like a wish than a measurable output.
  + Mention who will benefit.

## Project purpose

The contribution of the project to reach the Overall Goal and at the same time a new state/quality reached when envisaged beneficiaries adopt/utilise the project result(s). It gives an idea to the reader of what problem your project intends to address.

Framing a quality ‘Purpose’:

* + Keep only one Purpose for a proposal
  + Do not write a vague Purpose
  + Purpose should be in line with the problem statement
  + Purpose should be consistent with your organisations mission and vision statement
  + Use simple language to write a Purpose

Example:

|  |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Example | Indicator | Verification |
| Increasing local crop production by introducing a new water supply system to Burkina Faso | Volume contribution of locally produced crops’ | Monthly market reports |

## Project outcomes

The ‘success factors’ through which the value of the project can be measured. Outcomes are something that the project wants either to maximise or minimise”. Outcomes are the events, occurrences, or changes in conditions, behaviour, or attitudes that indicate progress toward a project’s goal or that reflect a positive impact to the target group.

Framing quality ‘Outcomes’:

* + Specific and measurable: tracking data to monitor outcomes is practical and timely.
  + Meaningful: Achieving an outcome indicates fulfilment of purpose and program toward long-term impact.
  + Generally begin with a verb like increase, expand, or improve.
  + Mention what is within and outside scope
  + Outcomes are not:
  + Activities or processes (hosting an event is an activity, having attracted the hotshots of the industry is a result, increasing awareness is an outcome).
  + Immeasurable long-term change.
  + To be confused with project outputs (=Results described in the next paragraph)

Example:

| 1. Substantial increase of water uptake by local farmers | An average monthly increase of water supply through the system of 10% | Central water supply measurement system |
| 2. Increase of competence around rolling out the technology | The number of associated local employees of the company | Human resource statistics |

## Expected outputs / results

The specific outputs the project management should be able to guarantee

and through which the outcome(s) will be achieved. Services & facilities provided by the Project.

NOTE: Results are most often wrongly taken as the ultimate project goals and measured through result-specific acceptance criteria without having kept in mind the purpose and desired outcomes.

Framing quality ‘Results’:

* + Three/four Results that would help us in achieving the particular Purpose
  + Qualitative vs. quantitative
  + Clear, distinct (what, when, why, where, who): Target group, deadline etc.
  + SMART:
  + Specific (simple, sensible, significant).
  + Measurable (meaningful, motivating).
  + Achievable (agreed, attainable).
  + Relevant (reasonable, realistic and resourced, results-based).
  + Time bound (time-based, time limited, time/cost limited, timely, time-sensitive).
  + Results should follow a logic order
  + Are the results clearly formulated and stated as an already achieved status?
  + Are each of the results necessary for sustaining the purpose?
  + Are the results in their totality sufficient for sustaining the purpose?
  + Is it realistic to assume that the project manager can be held responsible for achieving the results?

Example:

|  |
| --- |
| 1.1 Having a working water supply system realised by the end of 2020  1.2 Having introduced our product to 5 large villages in Burkina Faso by the end of 2021  2.1 Having a business growth plan in place by the end of 2020  2.2 Having human resource system in place  2.3 Having established collaborations with local employment agencies |

## Activities and required inputs

Activities are measures / tasks carried out by the project in order to achieve or sustain the project results. Inputs are the attributes to be used by the project (people, goods, services).

Framing quality ‘Activities and Inputs’:

* + Besides activities and inputs this section should describe any relevant strategy, outlined methodology, timeline and costs.
  + How will the project be carried out?
  + Any innovative approaches?
  + Ensure your project’s goals, objectives and strategies/methods relate to each other.
  + Each activity should be linked to a Result.
  + When will things get done?
  + What will each result cost? if you cant estimate cost then it is not an activity
  + Are each of the activities necessary for achieving the respective output?
  + Are the activities in their totality sufficient for achieving the respective output?
